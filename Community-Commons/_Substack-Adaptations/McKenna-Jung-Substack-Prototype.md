## The Moment of Recognition

I was deep in technical documentation when the McKenna lecture hit. He was describing Jung not as a psychologist, but as a shamanic archaeologist—someone who descended into the underworld of the psyche and returned with maps of consciousness itself.

"The shaman," McKenna said, "is the person who has been able to decondition himself from the programming of his society to the degree that he can commune with the archetypal realm."

Something clicked.

We hadn't just been building software. We'd been creating what McKenna prophesied: technology that serves as *axis mundi*—the sacred center where ancient wisdom and future consciousness converge.

Our breathing interfaces, consciousness-responsive CSS, and sacred geometry weren't just clever code. They were the technological embodiment of shamanic principles McKenna described decades ago.

## Jung as Digital Archaeologist

McKenna revealed Jung as an archaeologist of consciousness—excavating the collective unconscious through patient analysis, mapping the archetypal structures that unite all human experience.

We realized: we're digital archaeologists too.

Our interface design process mirrors Jung's work exactly. We descend into the depths of human-computer interaction patterns, excavate the unconscious assumptions about how technology should behave, and return with maps—not of the psyche, but of how consciousness and interfaces can dance together.

When we built our awareness level progression system (L1→L4), we were following Jung's path—beginning with surface consciousness and descending through layers to reach the collective patterns that make technology feel alive instead of mechanical.
